Detailed analysis of the influence of the environment on thermal stress requires knowledge of the following four basic parameters: air temperature, mean radiant temperature, air speed and absolute humidity. The WBGT combines the measurement of two derived parameters, natural wet-bulb temperature (Tnwb) and the black globe temperature (Tg), and, in some situations, the measurement of the basic parameter, dry-bulb (air) temperature (Tdb). The WBGT formula can utilize either ℉ or ℃, as long as usage is consistent. During periods of hot weather, the WBGT for outdoor conditions is calculated with the below equation, and the appropriate Heat Stress Risk and Flag Color can be determined using the table that follows.

WBGTO = 0.7(Tnwb) + 0.2(Tg) + 0.1(Tdb)

The following table provides heat stress stages (flag colors), temperature ranges, and recommended work/rest cycles and water intake for acclimatized individuals performing easy, moderate, heavy, and very heavy workloads. The process of acclimatization is characterized by a series of physiological adjustments that occur when an individual is exposed to a target climate, which effectively minimizes the risk of thermal injury. The requirement for acclimatization is agnostic of individual physical condition. Personnel are considered acclimatized after they have undertaken gradually-increasing durations of continuous work or exercise, over a seven- to 14-day period, in the same climate as the work activity of interest.

Wearing a second layer of clothing, such as chemical protective gear or firefighting bunker gear, adds 6℃/10℉ to the WBGT measurement for easy work and 12℃/20℉ for moderate/hard work. Additionally, wear of body armor adds an additional 3℃/5℉ to the WBGT measurement. This can, potentially, drive a shift from low or moderate to extreme risk of heat injury.
